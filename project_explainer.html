The Problem

What is de novo sequencing?

A mass spectrometer shatters a peptide into fragment ions and records their masses. De novo sequencing means recovering the amino-acid sequence from that spectrum alone — without searching a database of known proteins.

01
The spectrum

A peptide fragments along its backbone producing b-ions and y-ions. The mass spectrometer records each fragment's m/z ratio and intensity — a sparse, noisy fingerprint of the original sequence.

02
The challenge

Database search methods fail on novel organisms — if the protein isn't in the reference proteome, it can't be found. Wastewater, gut microbiomes, new pathogens: all require de novo approaches.

03
Our approach

We treat sequencing as iterative denoising. Start with all positions masked, run a learned reverse diffusion process conditioned on the spectrum, and recover the amino-acid sequence step by step.

The Science

From shattered molecules
to amino acid sequences

Understanding this project requires three things: how mass spectrometry works, why the standard database-lookup approach fails on novel organisms, and why a diffusion model is the right architecture for this problem.

Step 1: The mass spectrometer shatters a peptide

A proteomic sample — say, cell-free proteins extracted from a wastewater stream — is digested with trypsin into short fragments called peptides (typically 7–30 amino acids). These are then injected into a mass spectrometer. In the MS2 (tandem MS) stage, each peptide is isolated and deliberately fragmented along its peptide backbone.

Fragmentation is not random: it preferentially breaks the amide bonds that link amino acids together. This produces two complementary ion series. B-ions carry charge from the N-terminus of the peptide; y-ions carry charge from the C-terminus. If the peptide is A-B-C-D-E, then b₁ is the mass of A alone, b₂ is A+B, and so on. Simultaneously, y₁ is E, y₂ is D+E, and so on. Together they form a ladder of masses that encodes the entire sequence.

The spectrometer measures the mass-to-charge ratio (m/z) and intensity of each fragment. The result is a spectrum: a sparse set of peaks, each peak corresponding (in theory) to one b- or y-ion. In practice, many expected peaks are missing (unfragmented), many unexpected peaks appear (noise, neutral losses, charge states), and peak intensities vary widely with no simple physical model.

Step 2: Why database search fails at scale

Traditional proteomics software (Mascot, MaxQuant, Sequest) matches observed spectra to theoretical spectra computed from a protein database. If the protein isn't in the database, it cannot be identified — full stop.

This is catastrophic for environmental and metaproteomic samples. Wastewater contains hundreds of microbial species, many unsequenced. A wastewater proteome database would be enormous, computationally intractable to search, and still incomplete. Gut microbiome and ancient proteomics face the same wall. De novo sequencing is the only principled path to identification in these settings.

Existing de novo tools (DeepNovo, Casanovo, InstaNovo) use autoregressive decoding — they predict amino acids one at a time, left to right, like a language model. This introduces an exposure bias: the model trains on ground-truth prefixes but at test time must condition on its own (potentially wrong) predictions. Errors compound. The beam search tries to mitigate this, but it is fundamentally a sequential, greedy process.

Step 3: Why diffusion is a better fit

Absorbing multinomial diffusion treats the sequence as a whole. At timestep T=500, every amino acid is replaced with a special MASK token. The model learns to denoise — to predict the original sequence from increasingly noisy versions. At inference, it starts fully masked and iteratively refines all positions simultaneously.

This matters because amino acid positions are not independent. The identity of position 7 in a peptide constrains what positions 4 and 12 can be, because real proteins have evolved conserved motifs, secondary structure preferences, and hydrophobicity patterns. An autoregressive model must infer all of this from a left-to-right pass. A diffusion model can instead let all positions "talk to each other" across every denoising step through the transformer's global attention.

Our key addition — self-conditioning — amplifies this effect further. At each reverse step, we first do a fast preview pass to get a rough estimate x̂₀, then immediately use that estimate as additional context for the real denoising pass. This gives the model a global roadmap before committing to local residue decisions, adding +14.6 pp peptide accuracy — the single largest gain in the whole project.

Why it works — PeakEncoder

Physics-informed spectrum encoding

The PeakEncoder doesn't just project peak intensities. It explicitly encodes the complementary b/y-ion pairing relationship using a learnable scalar bias initialized at zero — so the model starts purely data-driven but can learn the physics of peptide fragmentation during training. Remove it and AA accuracy drops from 76% to 42%.

Why it works — self-conditioning

Global context before local decisions

In a 20-residue peptide, the correct identity of residue 10 can depend on residues 1 and 19. Self-conditioning gives the model a coarse global estimate of the whole sequence before it commits to individual positions. The +14.6 pp gain is not from extra compute — it's from better information flow.

Why it works — CFID+SGIR reranking

Using the spectrum twice

The diffusion model generates K=8 candidate sequences via beam search. CFID then scores each candidate by comparing its theoretical fragment ion distribution to the observed spectrum. SGIR adds intensity-weighted matching. Together they break ties that the model's own confidence scores cannot resolve.

Why mass correction failed

Post-hoc filtering ≠ better training

We tried discarding candidates whose predicted mass disagreed with the precursor by more than 50 ppm. It hurt by 9.6 pp: the model is not yet precise enough that "mass-incorrect" reliably means "sequence-incorrect." The right fix is mass-conditioned training — a future direction.

Architecture

Three targeted contributions
on top of absorbing diffusion

Each contribution is independently ablated. Together they take the model from 41.88% AA recall (plain absorbing diffusion) to 76.00%.

Input
MS/MS Spectrum

N peaks (m/z, intensity), precursor mass M, charge z. Sorted by m/z, capped at 800 peaks.

① PeakEncoder
B/Y-Ion Pair Bias

Sinusoidal m/z encoding + log-intensity projection. AlphaFold3-inspired learnable scalar bias on complementary ion pairs.

Ã_ij = A_ij + s·𝟙[|mᵢ+mⱼ−M'| < 50ppm]
s initialized to 0 — physics-informed but safe
② Diffusion
Absorbing + Self-Conditioning

T=500 cosine-schedule steps. At each step, a preview pass produces a coarse estimate fed back as additional context.

x̂₀ = fθ(xₜ, S, x̂₀ᵖʳᵉᵛ)
+14.6 pp peptide accuracy — biggest single gain
③ Inference
CFID + SGIR Reranking

Beam search K=8 with knapsack mass filter. Rerank by cosine similarity of theoretical vs. observed fragment ion distributions, weighted by peak intensity.

score(c) = CFID(c) + λΣᵢIᵢ
Pareto-optimal: best pep acc, lower variance

Real-World Application

Wastewater metaproteomics:
no reference proteome

Six peptides from a mixed-community wastewater sample (Sample 2) survived 5% FDR filtering and replicate-consistency checks. ESM-2 pseudo-perplexity confirms structural plausibility — all z-scores below 1.0 relative to the E. coli reference distribution.

ESM-2 PPL by Group

Lower = more protein-like. ESM-2 trained on UniProt. Wastewater sequences are novel microbial peptides — higher PPL expected.

Key Interpretation

Diffusion V2 predictions (PPL 31.03) are statistically indistinguishable from ground-truth E. coli EV sequences (PPL 31.38), confirming the model generates structurally protein-like sequences.

Wastewater sequences (PPL 37.15) are elevated relative to the E. coli reference — consistent with their origin in novel microbial organisms not in ESM-2's training data.

All six wastewater sequences have z < 1.0 relative to the GT distribution — within one standard deviation, confirming structural plausibility.
