\begin{abstract}
This report describes Checkpoint 2 of our \emph{de novo} peptide sequencing project.
Building on the Checkpoint 1 LSTM/GRU baselines (31.51\%/44.30\% amino acid recall),
we trained a multinomial diffusion model over categorical amino acid tokens conditioned
on binned MS/MS spectra. Four components are added: (1)~a \textbf{TransformerDenoiser}
performing categorical reverse diffusion; (2)~an \textbf{entropy-adaptive mass gate}
that enforces precursor mass constraints with per-position tolerance tied to denoising
uncertainty; (3)~\textbf{spectral noise augmentation} during training for domain
robustness; and (4)~an \textbf{ESM-2 pseudo-perplexity} scorer, a \textbf{multi-model
ensemble}, and a \textbf{target-decoy FDR pipeline} for wastewater mzML.
Across three seeds, the diffusion model achieves $70.77 \pm 1.52\%$ AA recall on the
E.~coli EV test set, within 2.1 points of the InstaNovo reference (72.9\%).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\emph{De novo} peptide sequencing recovers amino acid sequences directly from MS/MS
fragment spectra without a reference proteome. This is necessary for metaproteomic
samples such as wastewater, where many microbial taxa lack sequenced genomes and
database search is not applicable.

Existing tools such as DeepNovo~\cite{deepnovo}, Casanovo~\cite{casanovo}, and
InstaNovo~\cite{instanovo} treat sequencing as autoregressive generation over a
fixed vocabulary. A shared weakness of these methods is that mass constraints are applied only at
decoding time or as a post-filter, rather than being integrated into the generative process itself.

We formulate sequencing as \textbf{discrete denoising diffusion}: the model
recovers a clean amino acid sequence from categorical noise conditioned on the
encoded spectrum. A precursor mass gate is applied at generation time so that only
mass-feasible tokens are sampled. This report presents our full Checkpoint 2
implementation, experiments, and results.

\section{Methods}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Diffusion Model (V-1)}

\paragraph{Encoder.}
Reused from CP1: a three-layer MLP ($20{,}000 \to 1{,}024 \to 512 \to 256$) with
ReLU and Dropout(0.3), mapping binned spectra to a 256-dim context vector~$\mathbf{s}$.

\paragraph{TransformerDenoiser.}
Given noisy tokens $x_t$, timestep $t$, and context $\mathbf{s}$, the denoiser
predicts the clean sequence $x_0$ in a single forward pass:
The input combines a token embedding \texttt{nn.Embedding(23, 256)}, a learned
positional embedding, and a sinusoidal timestep embedding added to all positions.
The decoder stack consists of $4\times$ \texttt{TransformerDecoderLayer}
($d=256$, $h=8$, $d_\text{ff}=512$, \texttt{norm\_first=True}), cross-attending
to $\mathbf{s}$ as a length-1 memory sequence.
The output projection Linear$(256\!\to\!23)$ gives logits of shape $(B, 32, 23)$.

\paragraph{Forward process.}
Categorical diffusion with $T=200$ timesteps, linear schedule
$\beta_1=0.001$, $\beta_T=0.02$. The marginal is:
\begin{equation}
  q(x_t \mid x_0):\; x_0 \text{ kept w.p. } \bar\alpha_t =
  \prod_{i=1}^{t}(1-\beta_i),\quad \text{else } x_t \sim \text{Uniform}(23).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Training.}
3,142 labeled spectra from both mzML files, split 70/15/15 (2,199/471/472).
CrossEntropy loss (\texttt{label\_smoothing=0.1}), Adam ($\text{lr}=10^{-3}$,
clip 1.0), batch 32 (\texttt{drop\_last=True}), 50 epochs on a Colab T4 GPU.

\paragraph{Inference.}
One-shot at $t=100$: random $x_t \sim \text{Uniform}(23)^{L}$ is passed through
the denoiser to obtain $\hat{x}_0$. The entropy-adaptive gate is then applied at
$t=0$ with $\hat{x}_0$ as input to produce the final sequence.

\subsection{Entropy-Adaptive Mass Gate (V-2)}

The standard mass gate uses a fixed tolerance; here we tie the per-position
tolerance to the model's local uncertainty. At position $i$, the tolerance is:
\begin{equation}
  \text{tol}(i) = 0.02\;\text{Da} \times
  \left(1 + 2.0 \cdot \frac{H_t(i)}{\ln 23}\right),
\end{equation}
where $H_t(i)$ is the Shannon entropy of the denoiser's softmax at position $i$.
A token $a$ at position $i$ is masked out if:
\begin{equation}
  \left|\sum_{j \neq i} m(\hat{x}_{0,j}) + m(a) + m(\mathrm{H_2O})
  - M_\text{pre}\right| \geq \text{tol}(i),
\end{equation}
with $m(\cdot)$ the monoisotopic residue mass and $M_\text{pre}$ the neutral
precursor mass. If all amino acids are masked, tolerance relaxes to 0.1~Da.
PAD/SOS/EOS always pass. Gate confidence (fraction of positions where the
tight 0.02~Da gate held) is recorded per spectrum.

Neither InstaNovo+~\cite{instanovo} nor $\pi$-PrimeNovo~\cite{primenovo} couples
per-position generation uncertainty to mass constraint tolerance in this way.

\subsection{Spectral Noise Augmentation (V-3)}

With probability $p=0.4$ per training batch, Gaussian noise proportional to
5\% of each spectrum's standard deviation is added before the encoder:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{\mathbf{s}} = \mathrm{clamp}\!\left(
  \mathbf{s} + \varepsilon,\; 0, 1\right),
  \quad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\,(0.05\,\sigma_\mathbf{s})^2\mathbf{I}).
\end{equation}
We found this step reduced the gap between clean Orbitrap training spectra and noisier
environmental acquisitions. Augmentation is disabled at inference.

\subsection{ESM-2 Pseudo-Perplexity Scoring}

ESM-2 (8M, \texttt{esm2\_t6\_8M\_UR50D}~\cite{esm2}) scores each predicted
sequence for biological plausibility using the one-fell-swoop method~\cite{ofs}:
all positions are masked in one forward pass,
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{PPL}(\mathbf{x}) = \exp\!\left(
  -\frac{1}{L}\sum_{i=1}^{L}\log p_\theta(x_i \mid \mathbf{x}_{\setminus i})
  \right).
\end{equation}
Lower PPL indicates higher agreement with ESM-2's learned protein statistics.

\subsection{Multi-Model Ensemble}

For each test spectrum, we collect top-5 diffusion candidates, top-1 LSTM, and
top-1 GRU prediction. Each candidate $c$ is ranked by:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{score}(c) = \lambda \cdot \log p_\text{spec}(c)
  - \gamma \cdot \log \mathrm{PPL}_\text{ESM-2}(c).
\end{equation}
$\lambda$ and $\gamma$ are selected by grid search on the validation set
($\lambda\in\{0,0.05,0.10,0.20,0.50\}$, $\gamma\in\{0,0.1,0.3,0.5\}$).

\subsection{Wastewater FDR Pipeline}

Four wastewater mzML files (two samples, two replicates each) are run through
the diffusion model without labels. FDR is estimated via target-decoy:
each spectrum's $m/z$ array is reversed, the same pipeline is applied to the
decoy, and
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{FDR}(\tau) = \frac{|\{\text{decoy PSMs}: \mathrm{score}>\tau\}|}
  {|\{\text{target PSMs}: \mathrm{score}>\tau\}|}.
\end{equation}
Identifications with $\mathrm{FDR} \leq 5\%$ are retained.

The diffusion model reaches 70.77\% AA recall, within 2.1 points of the InstaNovo

\paragraph{Gate ablation.}
Turning the mass gate off raises AA recall by 1.2 points (73.20\% vs.\ 72.01\%).
The gated model is physically constrained (predicted sequences are consistent with
the observed precursor mass), while the ungated model is not, making direct recall
comparison somewhat misleading.

\paragraph{Ensemble.}
The ensemble scores 45.82\%, well below the diffusion model alone. We attribute this
to the LSTM and GRU candidates sharing the same failure mode (repetition of frequent
residues), so pooling them introduces noise rather than complementary signal.

\section{Discussion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\paragraph{AA recall vs.\ peptide accuracy.}
The diffusion model reaches 70.77\% AA recall but only 13.28\% peptide accuracy,
compared to InstaNovo's 33.1\% peptide accuracy at similar AA recall. One-shot
generation at $t=100$ recovers the correct amino acid \emph{composition} but not
reliably the correct \emph{order}. Iterative reverse diffusion would likely help
but collapsed during development due to EOS/PAD token injection in the re-noising
step; one-shot was the stable fallback.

\paragraph{Gate trade-off.}
The ungated model scores 1.2 points higher on raw AA recall but produces sequences
that violate the precursor mass constraint. For any downstream use (database matching, FDR estimation, structural modelling),
mass consistency is required, so the gated variant is the operationally correct comparison point.

\paragraph{Ensemble failure mode.}
The ensemble was expected to benefit from model diversity. In practice, LSTM and GRU
both collapse to repetitive high-frequency residue predictions on longer sequences,
so their candidates are correlated rather than complementary. NovoBench~\cite{novobench}
showed that no single model dominates across all evaluation conditions; effective
ensembles require models with structurally distinct failure modes, which LSTM and GRU
do not provide.

\paragraph{Wastewater coverage.}
Six identifications at 5\% FDR is a modest yield for four mzML files. Our model
was trained exclusively on clean Orbitrap E.~coli spectra, and wastewater acquisitions
differ substantially in noise profile, charge distribution, and peptide composition.
Spectral noise augmentation during training partially mitigates this mismatch, but
bridging it fully would likely require wastewater-specific training data.
